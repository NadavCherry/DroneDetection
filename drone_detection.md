Detection and Localization of Tiny Drones and Sub-pixel Objects in Images and Video: Deep Architectures, Super-Resolution, and Integration of Classical AlgorithmsThe rapid development of computer vision systems based on deep learning has led to dramatic improvements in the ability to detect and classify objects. However, Small Object Detection (SOD), and in particular drones occupying only a few pixels (4 to 6 pixels) within a high-resolution image, remains one of the most complex and difficult challenges at the forefront of technological research. At these dimensions, the object lacks clear visual features such as shape, texture, sharp boundaries, or distinct color, and it can appear as sensor noise, optical anomalies, or compression artifacts. When analysis is performed on a video stream in outdoor conditions, challenges of dynamic background movement, lighting changes, complex atmospheric conditions, and computational limitations of real-time processing are added to the equation.A standard monolithic approach, such as running a sequential model from the YOLO (You Only Look Once) family without dedicated adaptations, is doomed to fail in these scenarios. Generic models tend to lose the fine spatial information of tiny objects already in the initial downsampling stages of convolutional networks. To achieve optimal results and provide a holistic solution to the requirements of detecting tiny drones, it is necessary to design a full end-to-end architectural pipeline. This system must integrate solutions at the image preprocessing level (slicing to patches, super-resolution), dedicated architectures for high-resolution detection (P2 heads), loss functions adapted geometrically for objects without a distinct area, and classical methods for motion analysis and background subtraction in video. This research report deeply analyzes the array of methods, models, and workflows required to establish an infrastructure for detecting extremely small objects, with the aim of providing a comprehensive picture of the current academic and engineering frontier.Physics and Geometry of Tiny Objects: Fundamental ChallengesThe definition of a "tiny object" varies depending on the application context, but in the computer vision world, and especially according to the MS COCO standard, an object is defined as small if its area is smaller than 32x32 pixels (1,024 square pixels in total). However, in cases of long-range drone photography or UAV Imagery, objects are often defined as "extremely tiny" or sub-pixel, occupying less than 16 square pixels (i.e., dimensions of 4x4 to 6x6 pixels), which constitutes less than 0.1% or even 0.01% of the total area of a modern high-resolution image.The first and most fundamental challenge in detecting objects of this magnitude is the spatial degradation within front-end convolution networks (CNN Backbones). Networks used for feature extraction, such as those that make up the backbone of YOLO models, are designed to increase the model's receptive field through convolution operations with a large stride or pooling operations. These operations gradually convert spatial resolution into deep semantic representation. For an object occupying 4x4 pixels in the source image, a reduction by a factor of 8 (a routine operation that produces the P3 layer in YOLO models) will shrink the object's representation to dimensions of 0.5x0.5 pixels in the feature map. This means that the signal representing the drone is completely swallowed by background noise and lost in the early stages of the network, long before it reaches the network's neck and detection head.Another challenge lies in the class imbalance during the network training phase. Since tiny drones occupy such a marginal area of the image, label assignment mechanisms in anchor-based or anchor-free models struggle to generate enough positive examples. As a result, the network receives a massive penalty signal on background areas (negative examples) and a faint reward signal on the drone, leading to a severe model bias in favor of predicting the background and ignoring targets.Object Size CategoryEstimated Area (pixels)Impact of P3 Layer Downsampling (Factor 8)Visual Representation CapabilityRegular / Medium Object200x20025x25 pixelsShape and texture features are preservedClassic Small Object32x324x4 pixelsBasic outline is preservedTiny Drone Target4x40.5x0.5 pixelsComplete erasure (sub-pixel representation swallowed by noise)The third challenge stems from the hypersensitivity of evaluation metrics. The classic metric for evaluating geometric overlap, Intersection over Union (IoU), collapses completely when dealing with tiny objects. If a network predicts the location of a large object with a single-pixel deviation, the IoU value will drop by a fraction of a percent and the network will receive a gentle correction. Conversely, for a drone that is 4 pixels in size, a prediction with a deviation of just one or two pixels—which constitutes a negligible error perceptually—will lead to a catastrophic drop in IoU (e.g., from 0.90 to 0.14). In this situation, the loss function will consider the prediction a complete failure (False Negative), causing sharp jumps and instability in gradients during training.Adaptive Slicing Strategies (SAHI) and Original Resolution PreservationOne of the critical engineering requirements raised by field reality is dealing with high-resolution input images (e.g., aerial photos of 4000x3000 or 1920x1080 video frames), while networks such as YOLO were designed to accept inputs in small dimensions, such as 640x640 or sometimes 1280x1280. Compressing a full image from its original resolution to the network's input dimensions destroys spatial resolution; a drone represented by 6 pixels in the source resolution will become a fraction of a pixel after the resize operation. The preferred algorithmic solution to feed information into the network exactly at its input dimensions without degrading resolution is using a slicing paradigm.The leading approach today in both industry and research is known as SAHI (Slicing Aided Hyper Inference). This paradigm operates model-agnostically and enables the detection of tiny targets in huge images by breaking the spatial problem into smaller parts.In the SAHI process, the large source image is not resized at all. Instead, it is systematically divided into an array of patches of a fixed size that exactly matches the network input (e.g., 640x640). Each patch contains a portion of the image at its original resolution (a 1:1 ratio), allowing the tiny object to maintain its physical size in pixels. These patches are grouped into a batch and fed in parallel to the YOLO model.To solve the object truncation problem—a situation where a drone falls exactly on the seam between two patches and is thus cut in half and undetected—SAHI implements a smart overlap ratio mechanism. It is common to set an overlap rate of about 20% at the horizontal and vertical margins between adjacent patches. This way, an object cut in one patch will appear completely within the overlap area of the neighboring patch.Following independent inference on each patch, the predictions are mapped from local coordinates back to the global coordinates of the source image. This creates a duplication issue, as the same drone might be detected by multiple overlapping patches. To merge these detections, SAHI applies advanced merging algorithms, typically Greedy Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS) or confidence-weighted NMS. NMS scans all bounding boxes overlapping beyond a certain threshold and selects the one with the highest confidence score, suppressing or deleting the others. This ensures a reliable and clean count of targets in the image.It's worth noting that for a YOLO model to maximize the benefits of SAHI inference, it is crucial to pre-train it using a compatible method known as Slicing Aided Fine-tuning (SF). In SF training, the dataset is pre-sliced into patches with the same proportions and overlaps, ensuring the model learns to detect targets near edges. Additionally, during training, it is recommended to regulate the intensity of mosaic data augmentations. Mosaic causes further image reduction, so for 4-pixel objects, one should reduce mosaic probability (e.g., to 0.3) and disable it completely in the last few dozen epochs.Super-Resolution: Pixel Enhancement and Representation UpgradingAnother option considered is using Super-Resolution (SR) algorithms to enlarge the entire image and increase the number of pixels representing the drone prior to network input. Recent studies indicate that despite its intuitive appeal, Image-level Super-Resolution using algorithms like ESRGAN incurs heavy computational costs and often generates noise and hallucinations that cause false alarms and degrade detection.A much more advanced and effective alternative is Feature-level Super-Resolution. In this paradigm, the image is not enlarged at the original pixel level (the RGB input remains unchanged). Instead, the enlargement and detail reconstruction occur deep within the network's latent space and are applied solely to Regions of Interest suspected of containing small targets.Models based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN) architectures, such as Perceptual GAN and SOD-MTGAN, learn to enrich the features of tiny objects. The generator in these networks takes poor, low-resolution representations of small drones and generates super-resolved features in the vector space, making them resemble the features of large, clear objects. The discriminator attempts to distinguish between these artificially generated features and those of real large objects. This Knowledge Distillation process forces the network's backbone to learn and reconstruct tiny geometric patterns without actually enlarging the image dimensions, thus preserving a processing speed suitable for real-time demands.Super-Resolution MethodOperation ModeBenefits for Drone DetectionNotable DisadvantagesImage-level SRReconstructing the entire image to a higher resolution (e.g., 1080p to 4K)Physically provides more pixels per objectMassive computational load, generates false artifacts (hallucinations)Feature-level SRImproving latent representations within the network via loss-guided learningMaintains high speed, enriches only targeted drone areasRequires complex training of GAN models and special loss componentsNetwork Architecture: Upgrading YOLO Models and Adding a P2 HeadGiven the nature of objects occupying just a few pixels, even when utilizing SAHI slicing or feature-level super-resolution, the network itself must be designed to retain subtle signals. Within the YOLO family (such as YOLOv8, YOLO11, and variants like HierLight-YOLO), there is structural flexibility to adapt the detection head to the target characteristics.The most dramatic and necessary architectural adjustment for detecting 4-pixel targets is the integration of a "micro detection head" or P2 High-Resolution Detection Head. In the standard YOLO structure, the network neck fuses feature maps from deeper layers P3, P4, and P5. The shallowest layer used for prediction, P3, represents a resolution downsampled by a factor of 8 from the original input. As explained, a 4-pixel object is entirely lost in the P3 map.By modifying the model configuration file (for example, switching from yolov8.yaml to yolov8-p2.yaml), the network adds a convolution path that routes feature maps from a finer reduction level, P2, downsampled by a factor of only 4. At the P2 level, a 4x4 pixel drone retains a critical size of 1x1 pixel in the feature map. The P2 head preserves a small, precise receptive field that includes edge textures, allowing for the extraction of extremely fine features without destroying them through deep, multiple convolution operations.While activating a P2 head requires processing a high-resolution feature map (e.g., a 160x160 layer from a 640x640 input), which may seem computationally demanding, researchers have found that applying separable convolutions and significantly reducing the number of channels in the P2 layer keeps the complexity increase minor, sometimes offsetting weights from unnecessary deeper layers. Approaches like DroneScan-YOLO incorporate dynamic pruning (RPA-Block) to filter redundant information in high-resolution layers, enabling operation at high FPS rates.Upgrading Upsampling Stages: Sub-pixel ConvolutionsIn the network neck, designed to perform multi-dimensional data fusion (often PANet or BiFPN-based), it is necessary to upsample deep layers to unify them with shallow ones (like P2). YOLO algorithms tend to use nearest neighbor upsampling. However, for single-pixel objects, this interpolation introduces severe aliasing noise. A proven alternative is replacing the interpolation function with advanced mechanisms like Sub-pixel Convolution (Pixel Shuffle) or the CARAFE operator. Sub-pixel convolution rearranges multiple information channels from the feature map into a 2D space, basing the enlargement on network-learned information rather than mathematical pixel smearing. This preserves the delicate geometric outline of the drone.Loss Function: Normalized Wasserstein Distance (NWD Loss)To rectify the IoU metric's weakness during network training, the leading analytical solution in literature is utilizing a loss function based on the Normalized Wasserstein Distance (NWD). Instead of treating bounding boxes as rigid rectangles, NWD models both the model's prediction box and the ground truth label as a 2D Gaussian distribution.For a box defined by center, width, and height $(c_x,c_y,w,h)$, a distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu,\Sigma)$ is created, where the expected value vector is the object center $\mu=[c_x,c_y]^T$, and the covariance matrix reflects the dimensions $\Sigma=\text{diag}(\frac{w^2}{4},\frac{h^2}{4})$. This approach determines that the most significant pixel concentration is at the center of the box, decaying gradually toward its edges, which perfectly suits the representation of tiny drones lacking clear rectangular boundaries.The 2-Wasserstein Distance measures the probabilistic distance between these two distributions. The mathematical formulation for two Gaussian distributions $\mathcal{N}_a,\mathcal{N}_b$ converges to a simple, continuous formula:$$W_2^2(\mathcal{N}_a,\mathcal{N}_b)=\|\mu_a-\mu_b\|_2^2+\text{Tr}(\Sigma_a+\Sigma_b-2(\Sigma_a\Sigma_b)^{1/2})$$Since this distance is unbounded, an exponential normalization function integrating a constant $C$ (often determined by the average object dimensions in the dataset) is applied to keep it between 0 and 1:$$NWD=\exp\left(-\frac{\sqrt{W_2^2}}{C}\right)$$NWD's greatness lies in its lack of rigid boundaries. Even when a prediction box is 1-2 pixels away from the truth box of a tiny drone—zeroing out IoU—their Gaussian distributions still yield a quantitative, precise Wasserstein distance. NWD provides the network with a smooth, reliable error gradient, guiding backpropagation smoothly to full convergence. Integrating NWD into YOLO networks has been shown to raise mAP by several percentage points, eliminate the extreme sensitivity of 4-pixel targets to localization errors, and allow NWD to be combined with NMS algorithms (like in SAHI) for smart merging of nearby boxes.Video Processing: Integrating Spatio-Temporal Information and Classical AlgorithmsWhen the input is a video file or real-time stream, the drone is not just 4 static pixels, but a behavioral motion trajectory over time. Extracting and mapping this motion are critical tools for distinguishing between a moving drone and static noise. Here lies the power of combining classical computer vision algorithms with deep learning networks.Background Subtraction in Static CamerasFor video captured from a static camera, classical background subtraction algorithms can be leveraged to focus the YOLO network's attention. Algorithms like Mixture of Gaussians 2 (MOG2) and Visual Background Extractor (ViBe) operate efficiently at the pixel level to learn and filter the background.MOG2 maintains a mixture of Gaussian distributions for each pixel over time. If a pixel abruptly changes color incongruent with the learned model, it is flagged as "foreground". ViBe offers an alternative approach based on rapid initialization by randomly sampling pixels in the immediate vicinity of each point. In studies handling drone detection (such as the Drone-vs-Bird dataset), ViBe demonstrated exceptional robustness against complex background motions like trees swaying in the wind or water ripples, successfully isolating the tiny drone area.Integrating these algorithms into a YOLO and SAHI pipeline saves massive computational resources. Instead of slicing a full 4K image into 30 different patches and feeding all to YOLO, the binary mask output of MOG2 or ViBe can be used as a Region Proposal mechanism. YOLO will only process SAHI patches containing pixels flagged as moving foreground, drastically reducing runtime and enabling smooth real-time video processing.Temporal Processing in Moving Cameras (Frame Stacking)In scenarios involving a moving camera, such as air-to-air drone detection, classical background subtraction fails because all pixels are in constant motion. An elegant solution is exposing the YOLO convolution network to the time dimension without resorting to heavy 3D-CNN computations. Frame Stacking achieves this: the system takes a sequence of three consecutive frames, converts each to grayscale, and assigns each frame to a different data channel, analogous to RGB channels.When YOLO processes this combined image, the initial convolution filters calculate a spatio-temporal gradient. They identify the "motion trail" left by the tiny drone across the three frames. Consequently, the 4-pixel drone is perceived by the model as a larger, broader trailing object, dramatically increasing detection probability.Morphological Transforms and Thermal Sensing (Top-Hat / LCM)In challenging lighting conditions or when video is integrated with infrared sensors, the drone stands out as a "hot spot". Traditional morphological algorithms provide a powerful filtering tool before the image even reaches deep networks. Top-Hat Transform and its advanced variants (like Ring Top-Hat) rely on morphological opening and subtraction operations, identifying tiny point-like structures while ignoring large bodies and bright background areas. Regional contrast metrics like Local Contrast Measure (LCM) calculate intensity differences between a target pixel and a peripheral pixel ring. These transform outputs can be fed into a YOLO model as an additional data channel (e.g., a 4-channel image: RGB + LCM), providing a geometric "attention cue" to the deep network with minimal computational overhead.End-to-End PipelineIn light of the above, the complete pipeline that yields the best results in detecting 4-6 pixel drones in a video stream is a rigorous, synergistic combination of all algorithmic layers.
